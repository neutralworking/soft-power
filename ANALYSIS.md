# Soft Power — Analysis and Development Plan

## What This Project Is

Soft Power is a modern epic poem modelled on Dante's *Divine Comedy* and T.S. Eliot's *The Waste Land*, set in contemporary Camden and wider London. It follows Jolyon "Joly" Dox — a 35-year-old record label A&R man grieving his brother Cosmo — through an urban inferno of gentrification, millennial disillusionment, and the attention economy. A narrator acts as chorus. The cast includes Norah Healy (fleeing a broken relationship in Paris), Kwame Kofi Kinsey (an Oxford dropout turned poet-programmer), WK Blue (an American rapper), Ruckenfigur (a masked lyricist — later revealed to be Cosmo), Delphine $appho, Destiny Vegas, Baron Ron Dox, Tom Eliot-White, and a web of characters around the Camden music scene.

From this poem, three further adaptations have grown: original songs, a staged play (performed at The Nines, sold out, 2017), and a game concept called *Unreal Albion*.

**The poem is a loop.** The ending (petrol poured at Sunset, screams) kills off the cast in the eyes of the world. They fake their deaths — mirroring Cosmo's earlier fake drowning in the Port of Spezia — and disappear. The narrator is the only one left to tell the story. His opening line ("Iced boots and coats defrosted in a hot Sunset") is a reconstruction: the last three days of these people's visible lives. The poem's final line feeds directly back into its first.

## 2. The Problem with the Middle

You said the main issue is the middle. You're right. Here's the diagnosis.

### What's happening structurally

Act One builds momentum across four scenes. You go from Joly's drunken grief at the wake (Scene 1) to Norah's Eurostar breakup poem (Scene 2) to Burial's explosive "Mask off" moment and Ruckenfigur's debut (Scene 3) to the poets' kitchen session ending with WK's vanishing in a power cut (Scene 4). Four scenes, four escalations, each one ending on a hook. By the end of Act One the reader is leaning forward.

Act Three pays it all off. The rap battle at The Albion, Cosmo unmasked, the eulogy, the final gig, the fire — it's relentless. The poem earns its ending.

Act Two is where the momentum stalls. Three scenes, each containing strong individual writing, but collectively they plateau rather than build. The poem goes from sprint to walk precisely when it should be shifting gears.

### Scene by scene

**The Fire Sermon (II.1)** — Joly alone on the canal at dawn. This is the right scene in the right place: after Act One's noise, the sudden quiet is effective. The Beggar is excellent ("Politics and lists and a feminism quiz / And cats and lists and funny little pics"). The problem isn't the scene's existence, it's its internal pacing. Joly's soliloquy before the Beggar arrives runs through poets, legacy, censorship, guilt, Cosmo — too many topics in too diffuse a form. The stanzas here are looser than anywhere else in the poem. Some lines read more like essay than verse ("Is expertise not mastery of thesaurus / and dictionary? Intelligence withdraws us from savagery"). The scene needs to do one thing powerfully rather than five things adequately. The strongest thread is guilt — "Would Cosmo be alive if I'd understood his words?" — and that should be the spine. Cut the rest or subordinate it.

**Portrait of Destiny (II.2)** — Two halves that don't quite cohere. Ron's "I feel no guilt" monologue is among the best writing in the poem. The repetition, the historical allusions used as self-justification, the mounting desperation — it works because Ron is performing authority while terrified, and the form mirrors that. Then the phone call, then: Cut to Destiny. And the Destiny section, while interesting as character work, feels like it belongs in a different poem. We get her backstory (Anna Bartholomew, Channing school, nominative self-determinism, Robert Greene in her internal attic) but none of it connects to the plot engine. She doesn't interact with any other character. She doesn't advance the story. In a poem this tight, a character portrait that exists in isolation is a luxury. Ron's half drives the scene. Destiny's half furnishes it.

**Real Friends (II.3)** — The strongest of the three Act Two scenes, mainly because Norah carries it. The kintsugi speech is the poem's most precise metaphor for what all the characters are doing: constructing personae to survive. Evangeline getting verse for the first time is a good structural move. But the middle section at Never's flat — MTV, cocktails, "space oddities and novelty grinders," cultural commentary — meanders. It's atmospheric without being dramatic. The scene's job is to get Norah and the group to The Albion (where Act Three explodes), and the Never's section delays that arrival without adding narrative tension. The stage direction "THEY ALL WALK TO THE ALBION" is doing the work that the verse should have been building toward.

### The core problem

Act Two's scenes are character studies where the poem needs plot mechanics. Each scene deepens a character (Joly's guilt, Ron's fear, Destiny's ambition, Norah's reconstruction) but none of them create the question that pulls the reader into Act Three. The reader needs to feel, by the end of Act Two, that something is about to break. Instead, Act Two delivers three portraits hung side by side.

Compare with what Act One does: Scene 1 introduces the grief and the world. Scene 2 introduces Norah and a parallel emotional arc. Scene 3 introduces Ruckenfigur and ends with a confrontation. Scene 4 introduces the poets and ends with a disappearance. Each scene adds a new element AND ends with a dramatic beat. Act Two's scenes add depth but not forward motion.

### What to do about it

**Option A: Tighten, don't restructure.** Keep the three scenes but cut each one to its strongest thread. Fire Sermon becomes purely Joly's guilt soliloquy + the Beggar (cut the essayistic middle). Portrait of Destiny either cuts the Destiny section entirely or gives Destiny a scene-ending beat that connects to the plot (she could be the phone call that scared Ron — she already is, arguably, but make that explicit and give it consequences). Real Friends cuts the Never's section to a single stanza of arrival and spends the reclaimed space building tension toward The Albion — Norah hears music, or hears a name, or sees a flyer for the Raptors gig, something that creates anticipation.

**Option B: Add a connective thread.** The missing ingredient in Act Two is a subplot that runs through all three scenes and creates urgency. The phone call that scares Ron is the obvious candidate. If whatever Ron learned on that call has consequences that ripple through the other scenes — Joly hears something on the canal, Norah encounters something on Archway Road — then Act Two becomes a slow-burn fuse rather than three separate portraits.

**Option C: Resequence.** Move The Joker and the Thief from Act One Scene 4 to Act Two Scene 1. This puts the WK disappearance (power cut) at the start of Act Two rather than the end of Act One, which means Act Two opens with a mystery (where did WK go?) rather than a hangover. Fire Sermon becomes II.2, which works because Joly's dawn walk now follows the poets' all-night session. Portrait of Destiny becomes II.3 and ends Act Two with Ron's fear, which feeds directly into the Albion scene. Real Friends either absorbs into a restructured Scene or becomes the Act Three opener. This is the most disruptive option but it solves the pacing problem at its root.

My recommendation: **Option A first, then see if you still need B.** The material in Act Two is good — the problem is proportion and connective tissue, not quality. Tightening each scene to its strongest thread will recover a lot of momentum. If it still feels slack after that, add the connective thread (Option B) using Ron's phone call as the through-line.

## 3. Strengths

**Voice.** The code-switching between high literary allusion and street-level London vernacular is what makes the piece feel alive. "Stable hand on the table, said to be favoured / By Eliot in the 20s, later a blaze" — Camden pubs and Dante in the same breath, and it doesn't feel forced.

**Norah is the strongest character.** Mind Rain is the best canto. Real Friends proves she holds the stage in dialogue too. Her kintsugi speech — the idea that she built a fake self to absorb pain, "programmed with self-destruction codes / Rigged to blow and explode this new soul" — is the poem's most precise metaphor for what all the characters are doing: constructing personae to survive. And she's the one who reads Cosmo's eulogy in The Sympathetic Dead, which gives her a direct line to the poem's emotional climax.

**Ron's monologue.** "I feel no guilt" repeated three times, each time less convincing. The Alaric reference, the Lacedemon reference, the mother's gravestone ("Excuse my dust") — this is a man performing authority while terrified. Power dynamics in four pages.

**The Beggar.** "Politics and lists and a feminism quiz / And cats and lists and funny little pics" — the attention economy critique distilled into a voice that belongs in the poem.

**The Cosmo reveal.** The fight at The Albion — Joly hitting Ruckenfigur with a chair, the mask coming off, Cosmo missing all his front teeth — is visceral and dramatically superior to the earlier draft's quieter reveal. It earns the emotional weight of what follows.

**The ending.** Cosmo's final speech shifting from verse to prose, the petrol canisters, the Shantih line, the screams — and then the loop back to "Iced boots and coats defrosted in a hot Sunset." The fire/water symmetry with Cosmo's fake drowning. The narrator left to reconstruct the story. This is structurally complete and theatrically devastating.

**The Joker and the Thief.** WK and Kwame's verse exchange is among the poem's most energetic writing. WK's duality stanzas and Kwame's Oxford/class verse work as a genuine poets' session. The power cut ending is a perfect dramatic beat.

## 4. Issues (beyond the middle)

**Yoshikoko needs integration.** She appears in Burial, recites a poem, and Joly makes "a silent pact to visit soon at Albion." Her matchbook is the physical object that brings Joly to The Albion in Act Three (he's holding it when he walks in the door). That's good — it pays off. But she doesn't reappear herself. If she's a one-scene character, that's fine, but the "pact" creates an expectation. Either have her at The Albion in Act Three (even as a background presence) or cut the word "pact."

**Narrator voice inconsistency.** The narrator is "NARRATOR" throughout except in The Fire Sermon where "TYRESE" appears. Pick one. If the narrator is anonymous, commit to it. If he's Tyrese, name him everywhere.

**Destiny's isolation.** As noted above, Destiny Vegas has no scene where she interacts with other characters. She's introduced, developed, and then never seen again. Either give her a role in Act Three (she could be at the Sunset gig — she's a performer, it's a gig) or accept that she's a thematic portrait rather than a character in the plot.

**Some loose verse in Act Two.** The Fire Sermon has stanzas that read more like prose broken into lines. "Is expertise not mastery of thesaurus / and dictionary?" Compare with the precision of Mind Rain or Ron's monologue. Act Two's verse should match the standard set by the rest of the poem.

### Development Suggestions

1. **Create a song map** for the ten scenes. Each scene in V1 has a song cue (e.g., "Elleh Toledoh / 16 SECONDS", "Au Rue Boulet", "Shore Cutter"). Match existing tracks to scenes and identify gaps.
2. **Record demos keyed to specific scenes.** The play proved the material works live.
3. **Develop the character theme concept** as a standalone EP or album that companions the poem.

## 6. The Play

### What Exists

A staged production at The Nines (2017, sold out). Rehearsal photos, contracts, video. Two draft playscripts (PDF). The play adapts the poem directly, with Tyrese as onstage narrator.

### Development Suggestions

1. **The play is a separate work from the poem.** It should be adapted from V1 once the poem is complete, not developed in parallel.
2. **The production archive is valuable.** Rehearsal photos, sold-out flyers, and venue contracts are proof of concept for future staging.
3. **The play's ending works theatrically.** The petrol, the screams, the narrator left alone — this is strong stagecraft. A revival should focus on getting Act Two right (same problem as the poem).

## 7. Unreal Albion (The Game)

### What Exists

A D&D campaign planning document. Ten scene outlines. Character classes (Charismatic, Dedicated, Fast, Smart, Tough Hero). NPC stat blocks with musical combat. A combat simulation. Development notes for Ren'Py and Obsidian.

### Strengths

**The core mechanics are genuinely original.** Ego as HP, Creativity as mana, Attention as currency. This system directly embodies the poem's themes and could be the game's unique selling point.

### Development Suggestions

1. **Pick one format** — tabletop RPG or Ren'Py visual novel — and build a single playable scene.
2. **Formalise the Ego/Creativity/Attention system** in its own design document.
3. **Use the NPC template** (instrument/weapon + genre + signature attack) for all characters.

## Priority and Sequencing

### Immediate: Fix the Middle

Revise Act Two (scenes 5-7) using Option A: tighten each scene to its strongest thread.

**Scene 5 (Fire Sermon)** — Strip to guilt + Beggar. Cut the essayistic stanzas.
**Scene 6 (Portrait of Destiny)** — Keep Ron's monologue. Either cut Destiny or connect her to the plot.
**Scene 7 (Real Friends)** — Keep the kintsugi speech. Cut or compress the Never's section. Build anticipation toward The Albion.

### After the Middle

1. Polish Act One and Act Three for verse consistency with the revised Act Two.
2. Resolve Yoshikoko, Destiny, and narrator naming.
3. Game — build one playable scene using the Ego/Creativity/Attention system.
4. Music — record demos for three character themes.
5. Play — adapt V1 into a new playscript for revival.

### What Ties It All Together

The connective tissue is Camden, these characters, and one idea: creativity is both a weapon and a currency in a culture that commodifies everything. The cast's answer is to disappear — to refuse the attention economy by faking their own deaths and becoming nobody. The narrator's answer is the opposite: to stay, to witness, to write. The poem holds both responses in tension. That's the thesis. Every format should serve it.
