# Elleh Toledoh & Mind Rain: A Joint Enquiry

## T.S. Eliot & Ezra Pound in Socratic Dialogue on the First Two Cantos of *Soft Power*

---

## PART ONE: ELLEH TOLEDOH

---

**POUND:** Right. We haven't touched this one. Every other canto's had its surgery — this one's been sitting in the waiting room reading back issues of *The Criterion*, untouched. Time to open it up.

**ELIOT:** I confess a certain personal interest, given that the Sunset Club table upon which the protagonist steadies himself is alleged to be one I favoured. The poet has placed me, or my furniture, inside his opening scene. One is not sure whether to be flattered or alarmed — rather like finding one's photograph in a stranger's wallet.

**POUND:** Forget the table. What's the scene DOING? It's the overture. It has to establish the world, the grief, the brother, the ex-wife, the music scene, the Dante architecture, AND make you want to read Scene Two. That's a lot of freight for eighty-odd lines.

**ELIOT:** And therein lies the first diagnostic question. Does the canto distinguish between exposition that has been *made into poetry* and exposition that has been *arranged to look like poetry*? The answer, I'm afraid, is that it does both — sometimes within the same stanza.

---

### I. What Holds — The Scene's Genuine Achievements

**POUND:** The opening quatrain is solid work:

> *Iced boots and coats defrosted in a hot Sunset,*
> *A hole on Chalk Farm Road built in an old bunker,*
> *Acrid mist rising from the blackened wood, beading*
> *In fat pear-shaped tears resting on the low ceiling.*

Phanopoeia. Concrete. You see the room — the condensation, the heat, the underground quality. "Fat pear-shaped tears" is good because it's SPECIFIC. Not "drops" — tears, pear-shaped, fat, resting. The image does work.

**ELIOT:** I would go further. "Iced boots and coats defrosted in a hot Sunset" achieves something quite difficult: it establishes the physical setting, the season, and — through the word "defrosted" applied to human clothing rather than food — a faint suggestion that these people have been frozen and are now, at this wake, thawing back into something like feeling. That is an objective correlative operating at the level of the single verb. The poet probably didn't intend all of that, which makes it better, not worse. The best effects in poetry are the ones the poet discovers rather than manufactures.

**POUND:** And later:

> *The spirits mixed with the spirits to raise spirits,*

Triple pun. "Spirits" as alcohol, "spirits" as ghosts, "raise spirits" as morale. That's logopoeia — the dance of intellect among words. Three meanings in one line, and it's a wake, so ALL three are literally happening: drinks mixed, the dead invoked, people trying to cheer up. No wasted motion.

**ELIOT:** The Ginsberg passage, too, manages its allusion with more grace than I expected:

> *Saying he'd met some of the generation's best minds*
> *Destroyed by madness and absurdity, burdened*
> *By purposelessness, beholden to urgency,*
> *Too weak to complete their journey, instead twisting*
> *Listlessly through miserable purgatory, fists*
> *Vainly raised towards the Leviathan, lyres*
> *Strumming melodies aimed at powers dark and light*

The enjambment across "twisting / Listlessly" enacts the listlessness it describes — the line itself twists across the break and then lies there. "Fists / Vainly raised towards the Leviathan" works because the break isolates "fists" at the end of the line, a raised gesture that the next line immediately deflates with "Vainly." That is genuine craft, not accident. The allusion to *Howl* is transformed by the context: Ginsberg's "best minds" were destroyed by a hostile society; Warrington's are destroyed by their own purposelessness. The diagnosis has changed but the cadence carries the echo. This is how allusion should function — as palimpsest, not quotation.

**POUND:** Cosmo's letter is a neat bit of ventriloquism:

> *I've left because I wish to live deliberately,*
> *To front on only the essential facts of life,*

Thoreau pastiche, and it KNOWS it's pastiche — Cosmo is performing a literary exit, which is the point, since his death is itself a performance. The form mirrors the content. And then "me and Waldo are relaxing" — dropping from Thoreau's register into slang — tells you everything about Cosmo: he's read the books but he won't stay in their key. That's character through diction.

**ELIOT:** Tom's entrance is the scene's best moment of dramatic writing:

> *You were looking lost out there, old chum. In we go,*
> *There's a good boy. I know you don't partake per se,*
> *But Cosmo and I were Colombian brothers,*
> *So I feel it's only fair in his honour, eh?*

And then: *"We're having sex, fuck off. Are you finishing that?"* — which is perfect curtain. The scene ends on the only character who is entirely unselfconscious, and the bathos is the right kind: it releases tension without deflating the grief. Tom speaks in a voice entirely his own, which is more than can be said for some of the narrator's stanzas.

**POUND:** "A tear wells, like Orson" — good compression. Orson Welles, the man who staged the greatest trick in broadcast history, weeping. And Joly is weeping at a wake for a man who faked his own death. The Welles parallel isn't decorative — it's structural.

### II. Edits Applied

The following changes were made to `soft_power_v2.md` based on this critique:

**1. "All roads lead to Rome" couplet — CUT.** Dead idiom, "moreso" not a word. The scene moves cleanly from "But now with his glass empty and dry, it was time" to the stage direction.

**2. Mistletoe line — CUT.** "Like disco" is the right joke — a dead art form that won't stay dead. Don't follow your best punchline with a weaker one. Stanza now ends on "Never left, haunting the club's dancefloor like disco."

**3. Brothers passage compressed.** "The brothers, their relationship at its limits, / Visit at Cosmo's request and Joly's grimace / For this generation's version of Sunset's new dawn" → "The brothers came at Cosmo's request, Joly's grimace." Three lines into one. "Generation's version" was language from a marketing deck.

**4. Rhyme-chain halved.** Eight consecutive end-words on the same sound (reclaimed, disdain, constraints, shame, same, place, space, remain) reduced to four lines:

> *Cosmo alone had craved the fame, reclaimed*
> *His art and learned to disdain the family name.*
> *While Joly, knowing his place in the music space,*
> *Kept his collar tight, his playlist even tighter.*

Same information. Half the lines. The ear gets relief.

**5. "He had bigger problems to fix in any case" — CUT.** Prose transition sentence. "He had fallen out of love and grace with Delphine" is a perfectly adequate turn on its own.

**6. Nappy passage compressed.** Two lines into one:

> *Six weeks since he'd last changed her — did she still wear them?*

Preserves the joke. The question form puts us inside Joly's uncertainty rather than reporting it.

### III. Metrical Audit

**Lines where the metre is doing real work:**

> *Iced boots | and coats | defrost | ed in | a hot | Sunset*
> `/ / | x / | x / | x x | x / | x /`

Twelve syllables. Spondaic opening ("Iced boots") enacts the heaviness, the cold, the weight of arrival. Loosens through "defrosted in" (anapaestic substitution) before closing on stressed "Sunset." The music is native to the image.

> *In fat | pear-shaped | tears rest | ing on | the low | ceiling*
> `x / | / / | / / | x x | x / | / x`

Spondaic cluster in "pear-shaped tears rest-" forces the reader to slow down — to feel the weight of the condensation. The line's rhythm mirrors its content: heavy, slow, clinging. Auditory imagination operating correctly.

> *Here Jo | ly stands, | too scared- | stiff to | face the | jury*
> `x / | x / | x / | / x | / x | / x`

Opens iambic, then pivots to trochees after "scared-stiff" — a metrical stiffening that mirrors the physical one. Purposeful.

> *A tear | wells, like | Orson. | Prismed, | he viewed | a friend*
> `x / | / x | / x | / x | x / | x /`

Trochaic inversion after the caesura ("Prismed") stumbles, as a drunk man viewing a friend through tears would stumble. Then recovers to iambic. Recovery of balance. Craft.

**Lines where the metre falters:**

> *But now | a face | on ev | ery bus | on Chalk | Farm Road*
> `x / | x / | x / | x x / | x / | / /`

(NEW — the revision.) Predominantly iambic with an anapaestic fourth foot and a closing spondee. The spondee plants you on the street. Scans better than the old "incredibly famous" line.

> *He'd drift | ed so | far out | to Ber | mu dan | waters*
> `x / | x / | x / | x / | x / | / x`

(NEW.) Five clean iambic feet followed by a feminine ending. Textbook pentameter with the final trochee creating a falling cadence — drifting. Excellent.

> *His col | lar tight, | his play | list e | ven tight | er*
> `x / | x / | x / | x / | x / | x`

(NEW.) Five iambic feet with a trailing unstressed syllable. The parallelism gives the line its own internal music. Clean.

### IV. Remaining Issues for Consideration

These were flagged but NOT edited, left for the poet's judgement:

- **"Appalled but drawn to parricidal decisions, / Caught in static semantic disposition"** — the couplet is abstract. "Parricidal decisions" and "semantic disposition" are Latinate and heavy. They work if the intention is to show the narrator reaching for clinical language to describe what he can't face emotionally. But they sit between two passages of concrete imagery and the shift is jarring.

- **"Most talented malcontents, how woe betided!"** — "how woe betided" is archaic in a way the poem hasn't earned at this point. It sounds like mock-epic, which may be the intent (the narrator commenting ironically on Joly's business decisions), but the tone is uncertain.

## PART TWO: MIND RAIN — Deep Dive

---

**POUND:** The best canto. We both said so. The ANALYSIS said so. Now let's find out if "almost perfect" means "three fixes" or "thirty."

**ELIOT:** A preliminary observation. Mind Rain is the only canto in which the narrator, Tyrese, largely absents himself, ceding the stage to Norah Healy's unbroken voice — a dramatic monologue in the tradition of Browning, though filtered through a sensibility closer to Laforgue. The effect is that the canto possesses a unity of voice the others do not. This is not accidental. When a poem's best section is the one where the poet disappears most completely into a character, that tells you something about where the poet's gifts lie. Impersonality is not merely a theoretical virtue; it is, here, an empirical one.

**POUND:** Structure first. The canto has five movements:

1. **The Eurostar opening** — prose-comic dialogue between Norah and the Chump, with Tyrese's haiku-like interlude
2. **First love passage** — "Forever bound to you" through to "walk of shame"
3. **Three refrains** — *Dansons la capucine*, each marking a shift in emotional register
4. **The breakup** — "Here's the rub Prince" through the Pluto Shervington section
5. **The exit** — Chantal Sebire, "Forever, farewell"

The refrains are the canto's structural spine. They operate like a rondeau's recurring line — each return shifts meaning. First refrain: no bread, deprivation, the relationship is starving. Second: no wine, the pleasures have dried up. Third: pleasure at last, but only AFTER the loss. The French nursery rhyme becomes a chronicle of the whole affair. That's formal intelligence of a high order.

---

### I. The Eurostar Opening

**ELIOT:** The prose dialogue is well-judged:

> *They should really extend the Eurostar worldwide.*
> *It's the best way to travel.*

> *The Northern line goes to South London dipshit, do people get confused by that?*

Norah's voice is immediately established: confrontational, funny, too smart for her interlocutor, deliberately provocative. "Dipshit" does more characterisation work than a stanza of description. The Chump's reasonable objection — "It's called the Eurostar, it would be confusing" — makes Norah's retort funnier because he's technically right. She doesn't care. She argues for the pleasure of arguing. This is character through dialogue, and it is exactly what a dramatic opening should do.

**POUND:** Tyrese's interlude:

> *In the violet hour*
> *60 feet under la Manche*
> *She could almost taste Dover.*
>
> *Hyacinths and a sausage roll*
> *From Gare de Nord*
> *chug chug chug*
>
> *Norah Healy was in good spirits*
> *Complimentary in Standard Premium*
>
> *Girls across the aisle dance the Nasturtium*

This is the poem's only passage in free verse — short lines, no rhyme, imagistic. "Hyacinths and a sausage roll" — that's the whole canto in one line. High culture and low culture on the same plate. The hyacinths carry the Waste Land echo ("hyacinth girl"), and the sausage roll is from Gare du Nord. Norah inhabits both registers simultaneously, and the line enacts it through juxtaposition rather than explanation. That is phanopoeia and logopoeia working together.

**ELIOT:** "In the violet hour" is borrowed from me, of course — *The Waste Land*, "At the violet hour." One might object, but the borrowing is purposeful: my violet hour was twilight over the Thames, a threshold between day and night. Here it is the light inside the Channel Tunnel — neither French nor English, neither departure nor arrival. The allusion recontextualises the phrase rather than merely quoting it. That is how allusion should function.

"Complimentary in Standard Premium" — a double meaning (the drinks are complimentary; Norah is in a complimentary mood) that also captures the absurd class stratification of modern rail travel. This is logopoeia — the dance of intellect among words — compressed into a ticket class.

**POUND:** "chug chug chug" — onomatopoeia that also works as an imperative (chug your drink, as Norah is doing). Three words doing triple duty. Good.

---

### II. The First Love Passage

**POUND:** Here's where the canto earns its reputation. The passage is a single sustained arc from first meeting to breakup, told entirely through concrete particulars. No abstraction, no editorialising.

> *Forever bound to you, as the ancient saying goes.*
> *We held hands that August night down Rue de la Harpe*
> *(Possibly before it changed its name)*
> *Talking at length about our dreams.*
> *The words you said I couldn't say the same back.*

"Possibly before it changed its name" — a parenthetical that does ENORMOUS work. On the surface, it's a factual uncertainty about a Parisian street. Underneath, it's about the instability of names, identities, the self — which is the poem's central theme. And it introduces doubt into what seemed like a romantic memory. Nothing is fixed. Even the street might not be what she thinks it was. In one parenthetical aside.

**ELIOT:** The flash-flood tears passage:

> *Forever will it take me to forget*
> *Your flash-flood tears of practised anguish*
> *That crashed silently into an oyster shell,*
> *Before parting ways and wishing well.*

"Flash-flood tears of practised anguish" — the adjective "practised" is devastating. It recategorises everything that precedes it. We thought the tears were real; now we learn they were rehearsed. And "crashed silently into an oyster shell" — tears that crash but make no sound, landing in something that transforms irritants into pearls. The objective correlative is working at full capacity here: the oyster shell is a found object on a restaurant table that becomes a metaphor for the entire relationship — a container for pain that produces something beautiful. The poet has not explained this. He has simply placed the image, and it does its work. This is among the best things in the poem.

**POUND:** The paving stones:

> *That's the thing about good intentions,*
> *They perform the job of paving stones.*

Clean. The cliche is "the road to hell is paved with good intentions." Instead of SAYING the cliche, the poet shows the intentions actually performing the labour of paving. They're not ON the road to hell — they ARE the road. And "perform the job" has a workman quality that undercuts the sentiment. Intentions don't transcend; they pave. Concrete verb, concrete noun.

**ELIOT:** The Paris walking passage is where Norah's voice is most fully inhabited:

> *We smoked a fatty on the walk back from*
> *Montmartre to your flat in Batignolles,*
> *I'm normally nervous on a first date*
> *But there was something in the laidback way*
> *You pulled it out and gave it to me*
> *That I found impossible to turn away.*

The double entendre is obviously intentional and the poet has the good taste not to acknowledge it. "Pulled it out and gave it to me" works as both the joint and the sexual suggestion, and the line "That I found impossible to turn away" confirms both readings without choosing either. The tone is Norah's: amused, slightly reckless, more in control than she pretends.

**POUND:** The tourist stanza is sharp:

> *Two-ply the streets were trying to walk through*
> *In rush hour swelled with Asian tour groups*
> *Who pause to preserve every twist and turn*
> *Of the Seine to pretend to their friends*
> *That they saw more of Paris than them.*

"Two-ply" — thick, layered, also a toilet paper reference (Norah would make that association). "Pause to preserve" — alliterative, and "preserve" does double work: to photograph AND to keep from decay. The tourists are preserving a Paris that's already a construction. And the competitive element — "pretend to their friends / That they saw more" — is an attention economy critique buried inside a travel observation, which connects to the poem's larger theme without Norah having to know she's making the connection. That's structural intelligence.

**ELIOT:** The advice passage:

> *Which unfiltered cigarettes I should smoke*
> *And what folk I should avoid late night*
> *(Generally anybody with shaved heads,*
> *Long dreads or offerers of narcotics,*
> *Which in hindsight is ironic as it's*
> *The only advice that was correct).*

The parenthetical deflation is perfectly timed. The lover's expertise about Paris — breakfast spots, local haunts, cigarette brands — is revealed as fraudulent, and the one piece of advice that proved accurate was the most obvious and prejudicial. "In hindsight is ironic as it's / The only advice that was correct" — the enjambment across "it's" creates a momentary ambiguity before resolving. And the whole passage is a miniature portrait of the lover: someone who performs knowledge they don't possess, which mirrors Cosmo's performed death and the poem's larger concern with constructed identity.

**POUND:** "I don't know the French for walk of shame" — the canto's best single line. It does EVERYTHING. It's funny. It's sad. It's Norah admitting ignorance in a canto full of French. It closes the Paris section by acknowledging that the whole experience has left her in a place language can't quite reach. And structurally it bridges into the third refrain, which is IN French, as though the language she can't command for her own shame is the language the song chooses to celebrate pleasure. The irony is not stated. It is constructed. This is what I mean by the objective correlative — the emotion is caused by the arrangement, not asserted by the narrator.

### III. What Needs Attention

**ELIOT:** The canto's weaknesses are genuinely few, and they are concentrated in specific moments rather than distributed through the structure.

**POUND:** The hotel stanza:

> *Our hotel had sorts of cool inventions,*
> *A tad pretentious for my tastes but it came highly recommended*
> *By one of your wet mates from Rugby or Cambridge.*

"Sorts of cool inventions" — vague. The poet who gave us "flash-flood tears" knows how to be specific. And the second line is EIGHTEEN syllables. In a canto that mostly runs ten to fourteen, this line sticks out like a leg from under a tablecloth.

**ELIOT:** Suggested repair:

> *Our hotel had a bath that played Debussy,*
> *Pretentious — but it came recommended*
> *By one of your wet mates from Rugby or Cambridge.*

The bath playing Debussy is specific, absurd, and exactly the kind of boutique-hotel affectation Norah would find both amusing and contemptible. "Pretentious" alone, without "a tad" and "for my tastes," is sharper — Norah doesn't hedge.

**POUND:** The "Cecil Northcote Parkinson" lines:

> *You're no Cecil Northcote Parkinson, big shot,*
> *I'm not a resource you can delegate.*

The reference is to Parkinson's Law. But used here it's obscure in a way that doesn't reward the obscurity. "I'm not a resource you can delegate" works perfectly on its own. But "You're no Cecil Northcote Parkinson, big shot" doesn't work without the reference. Compare with "Molochs and angel-headed hipsters" — you don't need to have read *Howl* for those images to land. Parkinson doesn't.

**ELIOT:** Suggested alternative:

> *You're no corner-office god, you pompous twit,*
> *I'm not a resource you can delegate.*

Though the poet will likely find something better. The diagnosis is clearer than the prescription.

**POUND:** The Pluto Shervington passage:

> *I've heard the girls, now it's Pluto Shervington,*
> *Lord! Anybody see my trial? Grief for I.*
> *Why judge, I was badly beaten, found by*
> *Loved ones, battered by an irate husband,*
> *Searching for a man that was not I.*
> *Your honour was presented fraudulently.*
> *Your humour is offensive, not the good way.*

This is the canto's most opaque section. Shervington's "Dat" is being repurposed — "Your honour" becomes the courtroom judge. I can see the AMBITION — Norah channelling a Jamaican folk song into a courtroom scene that is also her internal trial of the lover. But the execution is knotted. "Grief for I" — is this Jamaican patois? A grammatical statement? The line asks the reader to hold too many registers simultaneously without giving them a foothold.

**ELIOT:** The problem is one of compression past the point of legibility. Compare with "Hyacinths and a sausage roll" — two registers, one line, immediately legible. The Shervington passage does not resolve. One reads it twice and remains uncertain whether one has understood it or merely passed through it.

**POUND:** Prescription: decide what this passage is PRIMARILY doing. If it's Norah's internal courtroom — keep the legal language, cut Shervington. If it's Norah hearing the song and riffing — keep the song's cadence, make the quotation clearer. Trying to do both at once is what's causing the tangle.

### IV. Metrical Audit

**ELIOT:** Mind Rain operates in a looser metre than the rest of the poem — closer to free verse with iambic undercurrents. This is appropriate: Norah is speaking, not the narrator, and her voice has a conversational rhythm that resists strict pentameter.

**Lines where the rhythm is doing genuine work:**

> *For ev | er will | it take | me to | forget*
> `x / | x / | x / | x x | x /`

Four iambic feet with an anapaestic fourth foot. The anapaest creates a slight hesitation — "me to forget" — as though the forgetting is being deferred even in the rhythm.

> *Your flash- | flood tears | of prac | tised an | guish*
> `x / | / / | x / | x / | x`

The spondee on "flood tears" enacts the crash of the tears. Then clean iambs through "of practised anguish" — the regularity of "practised" mirrored in the regularity of the metre.

> *That crashed | silent | ly in | to an | oy ster | shell*
> `x / | / x | x / | x x | / x | /`

The trochee on "silent-" reverses the momentum, creating a hush. Then the anapaest "to an" quickens into the final stress on "shell." The line enacts its own drama: crash, silence, landing.

> *I don't | know the | French for | walk of | shame*
> `x / | / x | / x | / x | /`

An iamb followed by three trochees and a closing stress. The trochaic sequence creates a falling cadence — defeat, resignation — and the monosyllabic final stress ("shame") lands like a full stop.

**Lines where the metre is slack:**

> *A tad | preten | tious for | my tastes | but it | came high | ly re | commen | ded*

Eighteen syllables. Nine putative feet. The line has no shape. It is prose that has been left-justified.

> *Your hon | our was | presen | ted fraud | ulent | ly*
> `x / | x x | / x | x / | x x | x`

The line cannot decide whether it's iambic or anapaestic. "Fraudulently" is four syllables that spill across the line's end without resolution.

### V. Specific Prescriptions

Seven items. The canto needs less surgery than Scene One — this is refinement, not reconstruction.

**1. The hotel stanza.** Replace "sorts of cool inventions" with a concrete image. Cut the eighteen-syllable line. (See suggested repair above.)

**2. The Shervington passage.** Choose ONE conceit (courtroom or song) and commit. The other can be a trace, not a co-driver.

**3. Line break fix.** If the hotel fix above doesn't satisfy, at minimum break the long line:

> *Pretentious for my tastes, but highly recommended*
> *By one of your wet mates from Rugby or Cambridge.*

**4. "Searching for a man that was not I"** — grammatically this should be "was not me" in Norah's voice. She doesn't speak in nominative case. "Was not I" sounds like a legal deposition, which may be the point (courtroom conceit), but it clashes with her register everywhere else.

**5. Third refrain comma.** The third refrain drops the comma after "capucine" that the first two have. Add it for consistency:

> *Dansons la capucine,*

**6. "The words you said I couldn't say the same back"** — syntactically ambiguous in a way that hinders rather than enriches. Suggested repair:

> *The words you said — I couldn't say them back.*

The dash creates the pause of inability. "Them" anchors the object. The line's sadness is sharpened by its clarity.

**7. "Lines printed to marginalise content"** — meta-poetic observation that pulls us OUT of Norah's voice and into the poet's. The first three lines of the passage are excellent. The fourth is the poet admiring his own metaphor. Suggested repair:

> *For every word I write another*
> *Seeks to follow, my pencil presses red hot*
> *Lead devouring paper. I can't stop.*

"I can't stop" is Norah. It's the compulsion of someone who needs to write herself out of the relationship.

### VII. Closing Remarks

**ELIOT:** Mind Rain is, as we have both acknowledged, the poem's finest canto — and the reason is not difficult to identify. It is the canto in which technique and emotion are most fully unified; in which the poet's characteristic gifts — for the concrete image, the loaded parenthetical, the register-shift that illuminates rather than decorates — are deployed inside a dramatic voice that never breaks character. Norah Healy is the poem's most complete creation because the poet has given her the freedom to be contradictory, funny, devastating, and wrong, all within the same breath. The weaknesses we have identified — the hotel line, the Shervington tangle, two or three metrical slippages — are blemishes on a surface that is otherwise polished to a high degree. The canto's greatest achievement is its structure: the three refrains, each shifting the emotional ground beneath the reader's feet, so that the final "Forever, farewell" arrives as both a surprise and an inevitability. That is the mark of a formal intelligence that knows what it is doing. One does not pay that compliment often. One does not need to.

**POUND:** Mind Rain is proof this poet can write at the level the whole poem aspires to. Every other canto should be measured against it. The fixes here are minor: a hotel line, a French comma, a tangled passage, a few syllables. An hour's work. The canto is the standard. Hold the rest of the poem to it, and you'll have something that lasts.
