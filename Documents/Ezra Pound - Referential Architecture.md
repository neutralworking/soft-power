# Soft Power — Referential Architecture

Analysis of the poem's layered reference structures, derived from the Annotated Manuscript footnotes. April 2026.

---

## The Six Architectures

The poem operates on at least six simultaneous structural parallels. Each line may be doing work in multiple systems at once.

### 1. Dante's *Commedia*
- "A Comedy in Three Canticas" (title page)
- Three acts = Inferno / Purgatorio / Paradiso
- "Elleh Toledot" = "these are the generations of" (Genesis) — same framing as Dante's opening
- "Midway upon the journey of our life" (Act Two epigraph = Inferno Canto I)
- "blackened wood" (fn12) = the Dark Wood, Inferno Canto I
- Joly is 35 = Dante's age (fn26)
- Maundy Thursday = the day Dante's Inferno begins (fn24)
- "Vexilla regis prodeunt inferni" (fn123) = "The banners of the King of Hell draw closer" — the four lines reference the four rounds of the Ninth Circle (Caina, Antenora, Ptolomaea, Jucecca)
- "Shantih" ending mirrors the Commedia's final ascent, but inverted — they descend into fire, not ascend into light
- Arnaut Daniel speaks in Old Occitan (fn57) — Rufio's "consiros ve la passada folor"

### 2. T.S. Eliot's *The Waste Land* (and Prufrock)
- Tyrese = Tiresias (fn3). "The most important personage in the poem, uniting all the rest" (Eliot's own note)
- Scene titles: "The Fire Sermon" (Waste Land III), "Burial" (Waste Land I)
- "Portrait of Destiny" = "Portrait of a Lady" (fn113)
- "burnished parlour" (fn114) = opening of A Game of Chess (Waste Land II) AND Antony & Cleopatra
- "In the violet hour" (fn31) = repeated throughout Waste Land
- "Hyacinths" (fn32) = Waste Land, The Burial of the Dead
- "measuring out her life with a silver teaspoon" = Prufrock's coffee spoons
- "oyster shell" (fn40) = Prufrock reference
- "Ephemeral performances unetherised" (fn126) = "like a patient etherized upon a table"
- "cloudy darkened dooms some two or three" (fn117) = Portrait of a Lady, line 10
- "Excuse my dust" on the gravestone (fn98) = "I will show you fear in a handful of dust" (Waste Land)
- "This is the way the world ends" (fn90) = The Hollow Men
- "April is the cruellest month" embedded in "This month has been too cruel" (fn96)
- Final line: "set our lands in order. Represent. Shantih." (fn130-132) = Waste Land ending: "Shall I at least set my lands in order? ... Shantih shantih shantih"
- The word "bullshit" (fn83): first recorded use by T.S. Eliot in "The Triumph of Bullshit." Second, third, fourth instances: Ezra Pound, Wyndham Lewis, E.E. Cummings.

### 3. Shakespeare's *Hamlet*
- Joly = Hamlet. Paralysed prince, dead brother, corrupt father
- "sickened hearts out the bitter cold" (fn9) = Hamlet 1.1.10, Francisco to Barnardo
- "yet unknowing subterranean world" (fn10) = Hamlet Act 5 Scene 2, Horatio: "Let me speak to th' yet-unknowing world how these things came about"
- "rotten state" (fn14) = "Something is rotten in the state of Denmark" (1.4)
- **"static semantic disposition" (fn15) = "As I perchance hereafter shall think meet to put an antic disposition on"** — Hamlet's decision to feign madness, rephrased
- "alack alack alack" (fn99) = A Midsummer Night's Dream
- "My salad days" (Portrait of Destiny epigraph) = Antony and Cleopatra
- Joly's first performance in Scene 10 = Hamlet finally acting

### 4. Nas' *Illmatic* (Track-by-Track Parallel)
Every canto corresponds to the equivalent track on Illmatic:
- **Scene 1 (Elleh Toledot)** = Track 1 "The Genesis" (fn5). "These are the generations" = Genesis
- **Scene 2 (Mind Rain)** = Track 2 "N.Y. State of Mind" (fn29)
- **Scene 3 (The Joker and the Thief)** — references "I never sleep, cos sleep is the cousin of Death" (fn54) and "It Ain't Hard to Tell" (fn55)
- **Scene 4 (The Fire Sermon)** = Track 4 "The World is Yours" — LINE BY LINE (fn70-80). Nearly every line in the canto references a specific line from "The World is Yours"
- **Scene 5 (Real Friends)** — continuation of Illmatic parallels
- **Scene 6 (Burial/Portrait of Destiny)** = Track 6 "Memory Lane" (fn95)
- **Scene 7 (Antigonish)** = Track 7 "One Love" (fn105)
- **Scene 10 (The Masque)** — "Represent" in the final line = Track 10 "Represent" (fn131)

### 5. Earl Sweatshirt's *I Don't Like Shit, I Don't Go Outside*
- fn73: "Earl is the Joker to Nas' thief in this book, representing the West coast (LA) to Nas' East (NY). **Every track in IDLSIDGO is referenced in the equivalent canto of this book.**"
- Fire Sermon: "Joly wallowed with bottle" (fn72) = "Grief" — "Find a bottle, I'mma wallow when I lie in that"
- "Good grief, it seems that I've been reaping what I sowed" (fn85) = Earl's "Grief" — "Good grief, I been reaping what I sowed." Written while Earl was ill.
- Earl's father Keorapetse Kgositsile (fn87) was a South African revolutionary poet who predicted violence would destroy the current, last generation of poets. This inspired the **Last Poets**, who released some of the earliest hip-hop records.
- Sympathetic Dead: "DNA" by Earl (fn121)

### 6. Fight Club / The Prestige / Identity Narratives
- **Tyrese PAULSEN** (fn3) = Tiresias + **Robert Paulsen from Fight Club** (who had breasts — connecting to Tiresias's transgender identity, "The Breasts of Tiresias"). Fight Club: men who fake destruction, create underground identities, burn it all down.
- fn50: WK's verse is a Schrödinger's Cat allegory with "Rick and Morty/Primer/Mr Robot references"
- fn51: **The Prestige** — "the third act of a magic trick." Two twins live half-lives. The Disappearing Man. "Ultimately neither succeeds... resulting in the demise of two." The Prestige is Act III of the poem — the reveal.
- fn52: "To be or not to be" (Hamlet) + "Are you a 0 or a 1?" (Mr Robot) + Camus' Myth of Sisyphus opening: "There is but one truly serious philosophical problem, and that is suicide"
- fn108: "Self-destruction is clearly the path to true salvation" = Tyler Durden

## Character Names as Etymology

- **Joly Dox** (fn23): "Doxa" = Greek for glory, praise, to appear, to seem, to think, to accept. **Joly Dox = "Beautiful beliefs"** or "Beautiful appearances"
- **Tyrese Paulsen** (fn3): Tiresias (omniscient transgender prophet) + Robert Paulsen (Fight Club — the man with breasts, whose name becomes a chant after death: "His name is Robert Paulsen")
- **Norah Healy** (fn35): maiden name of **Nora Barnacle**, James Joyce's wife and muse
- **Tom Eliot-White/Windsor**: T.S. Eliot, obviously — but the surname changes between drafts (White → Windsor), both suggesting English establishment
- **Ruckenfigur**: German art term — the figure seen from behind, facing away from the viewer. He never faces the crowd.
- **Rufio**: "il miglior fabbro" (fn56) — Rufio IS Ezra Pound in the poem. The master craftsman who discovers and elevates talent. Eliot dedicated The Waste Land to Pound with this phrase; Dante used it for Arnaut Daniel.

- "angelheaded hipsters" (fn18): Howl Part I Line 3

## What This Means for the Destiny-as-Commissioner Frame

The footnotes strengthen the case enormously:

1. **Tyrese Paulsen = Tiresias + Robert Paulsen.** The Fight Club parallel is not incidental — it IS the plot. Fight Club: men who fake destruction, create underground identities, and burn it all down. Tyler Durden's "self-destruction is clearly the path to true salvation" is QUOTED in the poem (fn108). Tyrese carries both identities: the prophet who sees everything (Tiresias) and the man whose name becomes a chant after death (Paulsen). "His name is Robert Paulsen" = "His name is Tyrese Paulsen." The narrator IS the memorial.

2. **The Prestige structure** (fn51): The poem itself is a magic trick in three acts. Act I is the Pledge (showing something ordinary). Act II is the Turn (making it disappear). Act III is the Prestige (bringing it back). The cast disappears. The poem loops. The prestige is that they were never really gone — they're in the narrative, forever, because Tiresias keeps telling it.

3. **Destiny's "burnished parlour"** comes from BOTH Cleopatra and the Waste Land's A Game of Chess. Cleopatra is the queen who chose self-destruction over surrender to Rome. If Destiny is commissioning Tyrese's testimony, she is Rome — the empire that arrives after the queen has burned. But she gets only the story, not the queen.

4. **"Represent. Shantih."** — The final line combines Illmatic's last track with the Waste Land's last word. If Tyrese is speaking to Destiny, "Represent" is hip-hop's command to speak for your people, and "Shantih" is the peace that follows testimony. He has represented. He is at peace. She has nothing.
